# Site Architecture Overview

## What is Site Architecture?

Site architecture refers to the hierarchical structure and organization of a website's content. It encompasses the way pages are categorized, linked, and presented to users and search engines. A well-designed site architecture makes it easy for users to navigate the website and find the information they need while allowing search engines to efficiently crawl and index the site's content.

A logical site architecture is essential for both SEO and UX purposes. By organizing your website's content in a clear and intuitive manner, you improve the ability of search engines to crawl and index your pages while providing a seamless user experience.

Implementing best practices for site architecture, such as creating a clear hierarchy, using descriptive URLs, and optimizing internal linking, can significantly enhance your website's SEO performance and user engagement. By prioritizing logical site architecture, you lay the foundation for a successful online presence that attracts, retains, and converts visitors effectively.

## The Benefits of Logical Site Architecture for SEO

- **Improved Crawlability:** A logical site architecture enables search engine bots to easily navigate through the website and discover all the important pages. By using a clear hierarchy and internal linking structure, you ensure that search engines can find and index your content efficiently.
- **Link Equity Distribution:** A logical site architecture allows for the efficient distribution of link equity (or "link juice") throughout the website. By strategically linking important pages and ensuring that all pages are reachable within a few clicks, you can boost the overall SEO value of your site.

## The Benefits of Logical Site Architecture for UI

- **Enhanced User Experience:** A well-organized website with a logical structure makes it easier for users to find the information they need. By grouping related content and using clear navigation menus, you reduce the cognitive load on users and provide a more intuitive browsing experience.
- **Reduced Bounce Rates:** When users can quickly find what they are looking for, they are less likely to leave your website prematurely. A logical site architecture minimizes confusion and frustration, reducing bounce rates and increasing the likelihood of users engaging with your content.
- **Improved Conversion Rates:** A user-friendly website architecture guides visitors towards your conversion goals, whether it's making a purchase, filling out a form, or subscribing to a newsletter. By strategically placing calls-to-action (CTAs) and designing a logical flow, you can optimize your site for conversions.

## Importance Of Logical Site Architecture For Technical SEO

A logical and optimized site architecture is crucial for efficient Google crawling for several reasons:

- **Discoverability:** A well-structured site architecture makes it easier for Google's crawlers (Googlebots) to discover all the important pages on your website. By organizing your content hierarchically and using clear internal linking, you ensure that Googlebots can find and access every relevant page, increasing the likelihood of those pages being indexed.
- **Understanding Content Relevance:** A logical site structure helps Google understand the relevance and context of your pages. By grouping related content together and using descriptive categories and subcategories, you provide Google with clear signals about the topic and purpose of each page. This understanding helps Google determine the relevance of your pages to specific search queries.
- **Link Equity Distribution:** An optimized site architecture ensures that link equity (or "link juice") is efficiently distributed throughout your website. By linking strategically from high-authority pages to important content, you help Google identify the most valuable pages on your site. This distribution of link equity can positively impact the ranking of your key pages.
- **Sitemaps and Indexation:** A logical site architecture makes it easier to create comprehensive and accurate XML sitemaps. Sitemaps provide Google with a roadmap of your website's structure and help ensure that all important pages are discovered and indexed. By submitting a well-structured sitemap, you assist Google in efficiently crawling and indexing your site's content.
- **Avoiding Duplicate Content:** A well-organized site architecture minimizes the risk of duplicate content issues. By using canonical tags and properly structuring your URLs, you help Google identify the original and most authoritative version of each page, preventing the dilution of link equity and potential ranking issues.

# How To Structure A Site

### Notes About Page Rank

Imagine you have a website. For simplicity, the Website has ten overall categories, and Each consists of ten pages. One of those categories is our 'top level pages' and includes the Homepage, the About page, and a bunch of other top-level content that is linked to on every page of the whole site.

Each of those pages in the top level category also link to a category homepage or index page.

The Category pages each link to 9 other pages about the specific product or service or whatever that the category is about. These are your second-tier content pages, and are the more specific pages for the product or service that the category focuses on.

So, that's 10 top-level pages, each linking to each other, plus to 9 other category level index pages. Only the category homepages link to the other 9 pages within each specific category.

Okay, so with that really simple structure in mind, imagine we have about 50% of all the external links to the site are pointed to the homepage, and another 50% in total point to various specific content pages or to category index pages.

Those exact ratios are not too important, the main thing is that we are accounting in some way for 100% of all the links pointing to your site, and thus 100% of all the link 'juice' that flows into the site.

You have 100 pages, and 100% of your link 'juice' value, pictured in your head, right?

Now, remember the structure. Your site Homepage is linking to 9 other top level pages, and also to 9 category level index pages. That's 18 links out from that page. So the 100% of passable value is divided between the 18 links, meaning each gets 5.56% of the total passable link value from the homepage going to those linked pages.

(There is actually a dampening factor, meaning that not all the value of links that comes to a page gets passed out again, but that a percentage is lost. The original patent had this at 15%, but the fact is it could be more, less, or even a variable amount. For now, we won't worry about the damping factor. For now just remember it exists, and is why direct links are still more valuable than internal links.

So each of the top level pages is getting roughly 5% of the total link power that the homepage has based on the link structure. Each of those other top-level pages are also linking back to the homepage, and to each other, and to those 9 category index pages, so each of those top-level pages also has 18 links in total, and is passing just over 5% of the value they have (5.6%) through each of those links. So the homepage is getting some added power from the fact that what it links to, is also linking back, but it is only 5% of 5% (or 0.25% of the total value that we said was coming into the site as a whole.)

The Category level index pages were also linked from every page, so they got the same 5.56% as the others, but each of those pages has 10 links to the 10 top-level pages, 9 links to the other category pages, and 9 links to the deep content pages in the category itself. So this time we have 28 links that the total value of links has to be split between.

That means each of those category index pages is passing 1/28th of the 5.56% that was flowing in, meaning roughly 0.2%, to each page it links to.

Remember, for the deep pages that are inside this category, not linked to directly from the rest of the site, that 0.2% of all the passable value is the ONLY real link value they are getting. That's all the 'juice' they have to work with when Google is working out the 'authority' style metrics.

If I create 10 new pages in one of those categories, so that it now has 20 pages in total in the category, then the category homepage that links to them now has 38 links instead of 28, and the link juice passed to each is now 1/38th of the 5.56% instead of 1/28th. Sure, you added new content, hopefully more keywords, and that is all great for creating relevant pages, but at the same time you dropped the PageRank score of ALL the pages in the category too.

Instead of each getting 0.2%, now each is getting 0.15%, or 25% less 'juice' than before.

### Logical Site Layout

Sites should be laid out in a logical order, from least specific to most specific topics.

Each site should include:

- Home page optimized for the brand
- "About Us" page
- "Contact Us" page
- Privacy Policy
- Page for each service offered
- Page for each city or location targeted
- Blog archive page
- "Areas We Serve" page (required when the site has more than 20 location pages — see hub page rules in the Internal Linking section)

# Site URL Structure Reference

The tables below show every page type defined in this SOP, organized by tier. Pages marked with a count condition are only created when that condition is met.

## Top-Level Pages

| Page Type | URL Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Home Page | `/` | `site.com/` |
| About Us | `/about-us/` (default) or a configured alias | `site.com/about-us/`, `site.com/company/about-us/` |
| Bio Page | `/about-us/[name]/` | `site.com/about-us/jane-doe/` |
| Contact Us | `/contact-us/` (default) or a configured alias | `site.com/contact-us/`, `site.com/contact/` |
| Privacy Policy | `/privacy-policy/` (default) or a configured alias | `site.com/privacy-policy/` |
| Services hub (when >20 services) | `/services/` | `site.com/services/` |
| Top-Level Service Page | `/[service]/` (flat) **or** `/services/[service]/` (nested) | `site.com/plumber/`, `site.com/services/plumber/` |
| Areas We Serve hub (when >20 locations) | `/areas-we-serve/` | `site.com/areas-we-serve/` |
| Top-Level Location Page | `/[location]/` | `site.com/los-angeles/` |
| Blog Archive | `/blog/` | `site.com/blog/` |

The classifier accepts either the flat or nested service URL convention. A site picks one and uses it consistently; the engine doesn't care which, but the client config declares the chosen convention so the canonical-conflicts auditor can flag any pages that diverge from it.

About Us / Contact Us / Privacy Policy paths are configurable per client because real-world CMSes regularly mount these under non-standard paths (e.g. `/company/about-us/`, `/contact/`). The engine treats the configured path as the canonical About Us / Contact Us / Privacy Policy page for that client and applies all rules accordingly.

## Second-Level Pages

| Page Type | URL Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Sub-Service Page | `/[service]/[subservice]/` (flat) **or** `/services/[service]/[subservice]/` (nested) | `site.com/plumber/24-hour/`, `site.com/services/plumber/24-hour/` |
| Local Landing Page | `/[location]/[service]/` | `site.com/los-angeles/plumber/` |
| Neighborhood Page | `/[location]/[neighborhood]/` | `site.com/los-angeles/los-feliz/` |
| Blog Post | `/blog/[post-slug]/` | `site.com/blog/how-to-unclog-a-drain/` |

## Third-Level Pages

| Page Type | URL Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Subservice Landing Page | `/[location]/[service]/[subservice]/` | `site.com/los-angeles/plumber/24-hour/` |
| Neighborhood Service Page | `/[location]/[neighborhood]/[service]/` | `site.com/los-angeles/los-feliz/plumber/` |

### Out of Scope

Fourth-level URLs (e.g., `/los-angeles/los-feliz/plumber/24-hour/`) combining location + neighborhood + service + subservice are not part of this architecture. If you're considering creating one, the keyword opportunity almost certainly maps onto an existing third-level page.

# Page Type Descriptions

## Home Page

Home page should be focused on the client's brand. Many sites make the mistake of optimizing the home page for the main keyword. However, the home page is usually the first place users navigate to, and so it should be optimized for that search intent.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## About Us Page

About us page should be about the company itself, not necessarily the services it provides. It should include the history of the company, the mission statement, USP, and some information about the owners or the leadership team.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Bio Page

Bio pages should go more in depth on the leadership team or the owners. Should include their professional accreditations, their work history, educational background, professional social media, and any professional organizations they belong to. This is used to build out the "authority" of the company and leadership team, as well as help potential customers get to know the company better.

**URL:** Bio pages live at `/about-us/[name]/` — e.g., `/about-us/jane-doe/`. Always nested under About Us.

*Please see schema [here](https://www.loom.com/share/ac51dd6eea3a48bb820fb40b8c67bb5c?sid=b3df8836-a5ea-4a2f-a8a4-fb0f5838aadd).*

## Contact Us Page

Contact us page should be short, there is no need to put hundreds or thousands of words on this page. This page should include the NAP, GBP embed, a form fill, and a click to call button or link. The page should also include the company's social media.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Services Hub Page

The Services hub page is required when the site has more than 20 top-level service pages. Below that threshold, services are listed in a nav bar dropdown and no hub page is needed. The hub page should only be about the services offered.

This page should be optimized just for the service keyword, not for the service + geo area. Copy on the page should show the expertise and authority of the company, list the services offered, and explain the benefits the service provides.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Top-Level Service Page

Top-level service pages should only be about the service. Unless the company only operates in or only targets 1 city, top-level service pages should never be geo-targeted. If the company does only target one single city, then the top-level service pages should target that city.

These pages should be optimized just for the service keyword, not for the service + geo area. Copy on the page should show the expertise and authority of the company, as well as the benefits the service provides.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Top-Level Location Page

Top-level location pages should only be about the locations the company is targeting. Each city should get its own page. If a company is only targeting one city, location pages will not be needed.

These pages should be optimized just for the geo keyword, and should include all major services in H2s. Copy on the page should show the expertise and authority of the company.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Areas We Serve Hub Page

The Areas We Serve hub page is required when the site has more than 20 top-level location pages. Below that threshold, locations are listed in a nav bar dropdown and no hub page is needed. The hub page acts as a repository for top-level location pages. When a user navigates to this page they will typically see a list of the locations served. This should not be optimized for any keyword except for "(brand) areas".

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Local Landing Page

This page is going to push the most geographic relevant power, and will be the one optimized for "(service) in (city)" keywords. It will also most likely be the page pushing the most power to the GBPs. These pages will be hyper relevant to "(service) in (city)" searches or "(service) near me" searches. These do not need to be unique from each other except for to be geographically relevant.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Sub-Service Page

Sub-service pages are second-level pages for more specific services the company offers, sitting one level under a top-level service page. For example, a plumber has a top-level service page at `/plumber/` optimized for "plumber" as a national/service keyword. They also want to rank for the more specific service "24 hour plumber", which is on a different keyword vector and needs different messaging. In this case, they would create a Sub-Service Page at `/plumber/24-hour/`.

Sub-Service Pages are not geo-targeted. The geo-targeted version of a sub-service lives at the third level as a Subservice Landing Page (see below).

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Neighborhood Page

These pages are for more specific areas within a larger city. Not every city will need neighborhood pages, typically just the larger cities that have recognized neighborhoods. If in doubt, check Google Maps and click on neighborhoods listed in the city. If it comes up with information on the left hand side with a description of the city, as well as other entities associated with it, it would be a good candidate for a neighborhood page.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Subservice Landing Page

A third-level page combining a location with a service and a subservice, e.g., `/los-angeles/plumber/24-hour/`. Optimized for hyper-specific keywords like "24 hour plumber in los angeles". These should only be made for highly competitive topics, or for topics/services/areas the client cannot rank for despite all other efforts. We generally will not need to go down this far.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Neighborhood Service Page

A third-level page combining a location with a neighborhood and a service, e.g., `/los-angeles/los-feliz/plumber/`. Optimized for hyper-specific keywords like "plumber in los feliz". These should only be made for highly competitive topics, or for neighborhoods where the client cannot rank despite all other efforts. We generally will not need to go down this far.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Blog Archive Page

This page should only act as a repository for blog posts. When a user navigates to this page they will typically see the latest blog posts. This should not be optimized for any keyword except for "(brand) blog".

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

## Blog Post

Blog posts will be mostly for informational intent keywords in order to drive nationwide traffic and build brand and authority, or to give more geographic relevance to the local landing pages. Informational intent keywords should never be geographically targeted. Blog posts about information about a city or POI or other geo area however will naturally be geographically targeted to that city or area.

*Please see schema [here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1C6K4hU_FPq6gsWyECaMyvs80IkYMJEK1t8dkzKivkJw/edit).*

# Internal Linking Overview

## What Is Internal Linking?

Internal linking is an important part of logical site architecture as well as SEO.

By strategically linking relevant pages within a site, you can establish a clear hierarchy, improve navigation, and distribute link equity (or "link juice") throughout your website.

This practice helps search engines as well as users understand the structure and relevance of your content.

Effective internal linking not only enhances the user experience by guiding visitors to related and valuable content but also encourages them to spend more time exploring the client's website.

## Why Is Internal Linking Important For SEO?

One of the primary benefits of internal linking is its ability to distribute link equity, also known as "link juice," throughout the site. By strategically linking to important pages, you can ensure that the authority and value of one page are passed on to others, boosting their SEO potential.

Additionally, internal linking helps create semantically related content silos, which are clusters of related content that search engines can easily identify and understand. By grouping relevant pages together through internal links, you can establish a clear hierarchy and context for your content, making it easier for search engines to understand the client's site more effectively.

Furthermore, internal linking is essential for enhancing user navigation, as it allows visitors to easily find and access related information within your website.

## What Are Content Silos?

Content silos are a way of organizing your website's content into distinct, theme-based sections. Each silo represents a main topic or theme, and within that silo, you create a hierarchy of related subtopics. This structure helps search engines crawl and index your pages more efficiently, as they can easily identify the relationships between your content.

The Importance of Semantically Related Pages:

At the heart of content silos lies the concept of semantically related pages. These are pages that share a common theme or topic and use similar keywords and phrases. By linking these pages together within a silo, you create a strong semantic connection that search engines can recognize.

Here's how semantically related pages contribute to the effectiveness of content silos:

1. **Keyword Relevance:** By grouping semantically related pages together, you reinforce the relevance of your target keywords. Search engines can better understand the context and meaning behind your content, increasing the likelihood of ranking higher for those specific keywords.
2. **Internal Linking:** Connecting semantically related pages through internal links helps search engines navigate your website more easily. It also distributes link equity throughout your site, boosting the authority of individual pages within the silo.
3. **User Engagement:** When users find relevant and interconnected content within a silo, they are more likely to engage with your website. They can easily navigate between related pages, increasing their time on site and reducing bounce rates. This positive user behavior signals to search engines that your content is valuable and relevant.

## Resolving "Related" Links

Wherever the body-content list above says "related X," apply the rule below. The goal is deterministic resolution — no guesswork at build time.

### Service Silo (Downward Links From Service Pages)

**Top-Level Service Page → Related Local Landing Pages:** Link to every Local Landing Page that uses this service — i.e., every `/[location]/[this-service]/` page on the site. No cap. Do not link to Subservice Landing Pages (deeper than one level down).

**Sub-Service Page → Related Subservice Landing Pages:** Link to every Subservice Landing Page that uses this subservice — i.e., every `/[location]/[parent-service]/[this-subservice]/` page on the site. No cap.

### Location Silo (Downward Links From Location Pages)

**Top-Level Location Page → Related Local Landing Pages:** Link to every Local Landing Page under this location — i.e., every `/[this-location]/[service]/` page. No cap.

**Local Landing Page → Subservice Landing Pages within this location/service:** Link to every Subservice Landing Page that exists for this location + service combination — i.e., every `/[this-location]/[this-service]/[subservice]/` page. No cap.

### Neighborhood Relationships

**Neighborhood Page → Related neighborhoods:** Link to every other Neighborhood Page that shares the same parent location. No cap.

**Neighborhood Page → Neighborhood Service Pages within this neighborhood:** Link to every Neighborhood Service Page that exists for this neighborhood — i.e., every `/[parent-location]/[this-neighborhood]/[service]/` page. Do not link from a neighborhood page to top-level service pages; the geographic relevance lives in the Neighborhood Service Pages.

### Blog Post Relationships

Blog post linking is governed by separate caps because blog posts are informational content, not commercial conversion pages, and over-linking dilutes their focus.

**Blog Post → Related service or subservice:** Link to exactly **one** Top-Level Service Page or Sub-Service Page — the service the post is promoting. Determine this from the post's target keyword, URL slug, and title tag.

**Blog Post → Related blog posts in the same silo:** Link to exactly **two** other blog posts in the same silo. Silo membership and topical adjacency are both determined by keyword overlap in the URL slug and title tag. Choose the two posts with the strongest keyword overlap to this post.

### Global Rules

- **No global cap on body-content links** for any page type *except* blog posts (1 service link + 2 silo blog links, as above).
- **Ordering / priority when many candidates exist:** manual priority. A human (the SEO or page builder) must set the order — this cannot be inferred automatically. If no priority has been set when a page is being built, the **senior SEO must be notified** before proceeding.
- **Placement:** in-prose links inside the body content are optimal. A dedicated link block between the page body and the footer (e.g., a "Service Areas" or "Nearby Neighborhoods" component) is acceptable as a secondary location. Both can be used on the same page.
- **Never link to the same page twice on the same page.** Google generally counts only the first link's anchor text, so a duplicate link with different anchor is wasted.

## Anchor Text Conventions

**Universal nav/footer links:** branded or functional anchors — "Home," "About Us," "Contact," "Services," "Areas We Serve," "Blog." These are UI elements, not keyword optimization opportunities.

**Parent links (child → parent in the hierarchy):** descriptive anchor matching the *parent page's* primary topic, not the current page's. A Local Landing Page at `/los-angeles/plumber/` linking up to `/los-angeles/` uses an anchor like "Los Angeles" or "our Los Angeles service area" — not "plumber Los Angeles." The parent isn't optimized for the service keyword, so don't point service-keyword anchors at it.

**Sibling links and child links:** exact-match keyword anchor matching the target page's primary keyword. A top-level service page linking to its local landing pages uses anchors like "plumber Los Angeles," "plumber San Diego," "plumber Santa Monica." A neighborhood page linking to its Neighborhood Service Pages uses the same exact-match approach. Consistency reinforces relevance.

**In-prose contextual links:** natural language anchors that fit the sentence, ideally containing the target keyword. "Our plumber Los Angeles team is on call 24/7" is fine; so is the more natural "If you need a plumber in Los Angeles, call us today." Use exact match where the sentence accommodates it.

**Blog post → service link (the 1 allowed):** exact-match anchor for the target service page's primary keyword. This is the highest-value anchor in the entire post — make it count.

**Blog post → related blog post links (the 2 allowed):** descriptive anchor matching the linked post's topic, typically the post's title or a shortened version of it.

## Click-Depth Target

All commercial pages — service pages, location pages, local landing pages, neighborhood pages, and their third-level descendants — must be reachable from the homepage in **3 clicks or fewer**. If a build would require 4+ clicks to reach one of these pages, the architecture is wrong, not the linking. Because fourth-level pages are out of scope, the architecture naturally satisfies this constraint.

**Blog posts are exempt.** As the blog archive grows, older posts move to deeper archive pagination pages and click-depth from the homepage naturally increases. This is acceptable because:

- Blog posts serve informational intent and authority-building, not direct conversion
- They receive most of their traffic from organic search landing directly on the post, not from sitewide navigation
- Internal linking from other blog posts in the same silo, plus the service or subservice they support, preserves their crawlability and equity flow

# Breadcrumbs

All pages on the site display breadcrumbs except the homepage. Breadcrumbs serve two purposes: they show users where they are in the hierarchy, and they reinforce the site structure for search engines via BreadcrumbList schema (covered in the schema SOP).

## Breadcrumb Anchor Text

Use the page's display name as the breadcrumb anchor — e.g., "Los Angeles," not "Plumber in Los Angeles." Each breadcrumb segment links to the corresponding page in the hierarchy. The current page (final segment) is displayed but not linked.

## Breadcrumb Structure by Page Type

When a Services hub page or Areas We Serve hub page exists, breadcrumbs route through it. When they don't exist (because the service or location count was 20 or fewer and nav dropdowns are used instead), breadcrumbs go directly from Home to the page.

**About Us, Contact Us, Privacy Policy, Blog Archive, Services hub, Areas We Serve hub**
- Home / [Page Name]

**Bio Pages**
- Home / About Us / [Person Name]

**Top-Level Service Page** (`/plumber/`)
- With Services hub: Home / Services / Plumber
- Without Services hub: Home / Plumber

**Sub-Service Page** (`/plumber/24-hour/`)
- With Services hub: Home / Services / Plumber / 24 Hour
- Without Services hub: Home / Plumber / 24 Hour

**Top-Level Location Page** (`/los-angeles/`)
- With Areas We Serve hub: Home / Areas We Serve / Los Angeles
- Without Areas We Serve hub: Home / Los Angeles

**Local Landing Page** (`/los-angeles/plumber/`)
- With Areas We Serve hub: Home / Areas We Serve / Los Angeles / Plumber
- Without Areas We Serve hub: Home / Los Angeles / Plumber

**Subservice Landing Page** (`/los-angeles/plumber/24-hour/`)
- With Areas We Serve hub: Home / Areas We Serve / Los Angeles / Plumber / 24 Hour
- Without Areas We Serve hub: Home / Los Angeles / Plumber / 24 Hour

**Neighborhood Page** (`/los-angeles/los-feliz/`)
- With Areas We Serve hub: Home / Areas We Serve / Los Angeles / Los Feliz
- Without Areas We Serve hub: Home / Los Angeles / Los Feliz

**Neighborhood Service Page** (`/los-angeles/los-feliz/plumber/`)
- With Areas We Serve hub: Home / Areas We Serve / Los Angeles / Los Feliz / Plumber
- Without Areas We Serve hub: Home / Los Angeles / Los Feliz / Plumber

**Blog Post** (`/blog/post-name/`)
- Home / Blog / [Post Title]

## Schema

BreadcrumbList JSON-LD is required on every page with breadcrumbs. Implementation details and schema markup specifications are covered in the schema SOP.
